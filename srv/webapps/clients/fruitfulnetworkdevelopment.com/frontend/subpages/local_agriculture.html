Local Agriculture Equity

A curated scan of Northeast Ohio organizations and practices that keep value local, expand market access, and build resilient food systems. Each card highlights a different model for equitable agriculture, from shared infrastructure and cooperative networks to waste-to-fertilizer loops and farmer-led retail

Historic

This section highlights Cleveland’s agricultural past, from its days as the Greenhouse Capital of America to the rise of wholesale food terminals and the pioneers who made local food a movement. Understanding this history provides context for current and emerging models.

Historic infrastructure

Cleveland’s Greenhouse Industry (Historical)

From the late 19th century through the mid‑20th century, Greater Cleveland became the “Greenhouse Capital of America.” Martin Ruetenik built the first greenhouse in Brooklyn Heights in 1885, and by the mid‑1920s about 50 businesses maintained 80 acres of greenhouses, growing tomatoes, lettuce and cucumbers. The industry expanded to 400 acres under glass by the early 1960s, employing roughly 1,000 hothouse farmers—many of them Puerto Rican migrants. Produce was sold through roadside stands, markets and the Northern Ohio Food Terminal, enabling restaurants and households to enjoy fresh vegetables year‑round. Cheap coal made heating affordable early on, but rising energy costs, pollution and competition from Canadian greenhouses in the 1970s led to decline.

Rise and Decline

Greenhouse farming was labour‑intensive and expensive, requiring misting systems, boiler‑heated steam pipes and careful pollination. The R.O. Apelt Sons operation grew to 3.5 acres by 1959 but dismantled its greenhouse in 2016, illustrating the sector’s collapse. Many operators switched to flowers or closed entirely as Canadian subsidies tilted the market.

  2. 1920s Approximately 50 businesses with 80 acres under glass.
  3. 1960s Peak at 400 acres and ~1,000 greenhouse farmers.
  4. 2016 One of the last major houses dismantled.

Wholesale market anchor

Northern Ohio Food Terminal & Sanson Company

The Northern Ohio Food Terminal opened in June 1929 on a 34‑acre site and once housed 100 merchants, employing about 1,500 people and handling roughly $140 million worth of food annually. During the late 1950s and early 1960s the terminal distributed nearly $200 million in produce each year, making it a vital organizing hub for regional agriculture. Produce arrived via railcars and trucks and moved quickly to wholesalers and retailers.

Today’s Role

While most of the original merchants have closed, Sanson Company— founded in 1914—remains the anchor wholesaler. Sanson provides retail, wholesale and food‑service customers with fresh produce backed by safety and quality programs. The company distributes local, organic and specialty produce to grocery stores and warehouses, supports local growers by purchasing their products, and operates a 25,000‑square‑foot certified organic facility.

Farm-to-table advocacy

Parker Bosley – Cleveland’s Local Food Pioneer

Parker Bosley, often called the “grandfather of Cleveland’s local food,” grew up on a dairy farm and studied cooking in Paris before opening Parker’s New American Bistro. Over two decades he built a menu that relied exclusively on foods produced by Ohio and nearby Pennsylvania farmers. His menu changed daily to reflect what was in season, and he visited farms to encourage specific varieties and breeds.

Fair Prices & Farmer Relationships

Bosley cultivated a network of cheese producers and small farms, paying farmers a premium (e.g., $10 per pound for artisan cheese) to ensure profits flowed directly back to them. He championed small‑scale, sustainable farming and challenged the notion that cheap food should come at the expense of natural resources and rural economies. His advocacy laid the groundwork for Cleveland’s farm‑to‑table movement.

“Local food isn’t cheap; it’s fair.”

Historic supply chains

Food Terminals & Produce Auctions (Historic)

In the early 20th century, food terminals were the primary organizing facet of local agriculture. Cleveland once hosted seven or eight terminals, relying on local farms to supply these bustling hubs. Produce trains from the West Coast—called “fruit blocks”—traveled east with perishable goods that often had priority over passenger trains. The Pennsylvania Railroad Fruit Auction & Sales Building in Pittsburgh’s Strip District, constructed in 1926, stretched about 1,533 feet long (roughly five city blocks). This massive warehouse allowed farmers and brokers to unload railcars directly into auction bays where fruit and produce were sold at dawn. Auctions began early so goods could reach stores and restaurants by opening time, a logistical rhythm that kept regional food systems running.

These terminals thrived thanks to the proximity of rail lines, but as refrigerated trucking became widespread, rail‑based produce distribution declined. Many terminals were demolished or repurposed; only a handful of operations, like the Northern Ohio Food Terminal (now Sanson Company) and the Consumers Produce facility in Pittsburgh, remain today. The history underscores how infrastructure shaped regional food economies and how transportation innovations can shift the balance between local and industrial supply chains.

  1. 1926 Pittsburgh’s produce terminal spans four city blocks.
  2. 1930s Cleveland hosts seven or eight terminals.
  3. 1960s+ Refrigerated trucking displaces rail‑based auctions.
  4. Today A handful of terminals still anchor regional wholesale.

Regional Food Hub Models

Regional hubs organize production, aggregation and retail to ensure independent farms remain competitive. This section explores cooperative food hubs, regenerative retail ventures and farmer networks that strengthen local supply chains.

Regional food hub

Oberlin Food Hub

The Oberlin Food Hub is a nonprofit founded in 2017 to build an efficient, equitable regional food system. It supports sustainable agriculture and small‑ and mid‑size producers while ensuring consistent access to healthy, affordable food for communities. The hub offers a centralized ordering platform and manages logistics—aggregation, cold storage, invoicing and customer service—so farmers can focus on growing food. It purchases products from area farms and supplies them to food banks, independent grocers, colleges, restaurants and farm stores.

Equity Focus

By pooling supply from many small farms and handling logistics centrally, the Food Hub creates fair market access for growers who might otherwise struggle to reach wholesale buyers. The organization emphasizes fairness, transparency and shared prosperity; it plans to expand services with a shared‑use kitchen incubator to process Grade B produce and build climate resilience.

Farmer-owned cooperative

Green Field Farms Cooperative

Green Field Farms is a cooperative founded in 2003 by Amish and Conservative Mennonite farmers and businessmen to revitalize family farms through sustainable agriculture. The cooperative emphasizes culture, sustainability and community, aiming to foster economic stability for member families while supplying fresh, nutritious food under a trustworthy brand.

Natural Fertilizer & Soil Health

The cooperative launched a natural fertilizer division that offers soil testing and custom‑blended fertilizers tailored to individual farm needs. These blends help over 1,000 farmers improve soil health and balance nutrient levels, emphasizing long‑term generational benefits. The program underscores the cooperative’s commitment to regenerative practices and provides an equitable alternative to conventional chemical inputs.

Soil Testing Access

Custom blends and nutrient balance tools let small farms compete without expensive inputs.

Regenerative retail

Purplebrown Farm Store

Purplebrown Farm Store in Peninsula sources food exclusively from Northeast Ohio growers and regenerative, organic and sustainable artisans. Founded by Sasha and Jimmy Miller, the venture began as Purplebrown Farmstead—a 12‑acre diversified permaculture farm established in 2016 within Cuyahoga Valley National Park. The farm grows mushrooms, pasture‑raised pork, eggs, cider apples, berries and perennial produce using permaculture principles, and is certified organic.

In 2021, the Millers expanded from a farm stand to a brick‑and‑mortar store to collaborate with other producers and make local food more accessible. The store aims to provide everything needed for nourishing meals while supporting the Ohio food and farm community.

Community food education

Harbor Gardens

Located in Ashtabula County, Harbor Gardens is a local food market and demonstration kitchen designed to build community around self‑reliance and food security. The general store offers products sustainably grown and made in the county and hosts more than 50 local vendors. Shoppers find seasonal produce, grains, beans, cheese, sourdough bread, maple syrup, handmade items and more.

Classes & Knowledge Sharing

Beyond retail, Harbor Gardens provides tours and classes on cooking, canning and fermentation, helping residents learn practical skills for preserving and preparing local food. This educational component underscores equity by empowering community members to participate in food production and preservation.

Vendor Mix

Community Kitchen

Amish grain network

Stutzman Farms

Stutzman Farms in Millersburg, Ohio, produces certified organic flours, oats and other grains using an on‑site mill. The family‑run business supplies organic granolas and flours to local markets and partners with about 20 certified organic farmers—many of them Amish with 10‑ to 15‑acre fields—to grow grains. Grain is milled with old‑fashioned equipment, and sprouted grains and gluten‑free options are available.

Partnerships with Amish Farmers

Stutzman Farms sells most of its products through Amish bulk food stores across several states. By providing seed and a market for small producers, the company helps sustain a network of independent farms. The farm’s puffer machine, used to make puffed cereals, illustrates how small innovations can add value to local grain crops.

Amish network

Yellow Bird Food Shed

Yellow Bird Food Shed (YBFS) is a distribution network founded in 2014 to decentralize the food system one customer at a time. Launched from founder Benji Ballmer’s house, it now occupies a 10,000‑square‑foot facility in Mount Vernon, Ohio, where staff receive, pack and store produce, dried goods and boxes for delivery. YBFS sources from a network of small, sustainable farmers so that growers are fairly paid and have stable access to markets. Unlike conventional CSAs, subscriptions are optional; customers who keep something in their cart every week receive a 5 percent discount.

Decentralizing the Food System

By delivering locally grown food year‑round, YBFS overcomes limitations of farmers’ markets and ensures that consumers can shop locally even in winter. The flexible box model—no upfront fee, customizable orders and weekly discounts—attracts more than 2,000 members. Each product is traceable back to the farm it came from, giving buyers confidence in the supply chain.

Specialization

Certain businesses focus on a single part of the food value chain— distribution, processing or staple crop revival—yet they contribute to equitable food systems through scale, efficiency and innovation.

Distribution

International Specialty Produce

International Specialty Produce, Inc. (ISP) imports hydroponic, organic and conventional produce from global growers and provides a one‑stop shop for wholesale buyers. The company, incorporated in 1994, direct‑sources fresh and frozen produce from Europe, Israel, Canada and Mexico. By 1999, ISP acquired a 60,000‑square‑foot warehouse with freezers and coolers and later added ISP Services, Cleveland Tofu and ISP Logistics to manage transportation and logistics.

Warehousing & Logistics

ISP’s warehousing services offer refrigerated, frozen and dry storage, cross‑docking, re‑palletizing, boxing, labelling, shrink‑wrapping and federal inspections. Redistribution options include full truckload and less‑than‑truckload services with 24‑hour availability. ISP Logistics helps customers minimize border delays, reduce detention pay and control fuel and toll costs, while 53‑foot air‑ride refrigerated trailers maintain quality.

Zero-waste processing

Keller Meats

Keller Meats began as a family farm and now operates a custom processing butcher shop with two market locations. The company’s mission is sustainability: they use the whole animal, strive for zero waste and support local sources. Keller Meats believes in building a community that knows its food and farmers and acts as stewards of the land.

Whole-Animal Stewardship

By processing locally and using every cut, Keller Meats keeps value on farms and lowers waste.

By connecting consumers directly to farmers and emphasizing transparent sourcing, Keller Meats fosters a deeper appreciation for ethical meat production and encourages responsible consumption.

Staple crop revival

Shagbark Seed & Mill

Shagbark Seed & Mill offers single‑source, Ohio‑grown certified organic heirloom grains and dry beans. Since 2010, the mill has connected family farms to grocers, restaurants, schools and health centers, and sells to food access sites at rock‑bottom pricing. Shagbark advocates for a “Staple Foods Revolution,” arguing that large seed and chemical corporations have reduced flavor, nutrition and climate resilience in staple crops.

Heirloom Staples

Single‑source beans and grains help local farms secure predictable demand.

Collaborative Network

Farmers, millers and bakers coordinate to keep processing and profits local.

Community & History

Founders Michelle Ajamian and Brandon Jaeger tested high‑nutrition bean and grain varieties with a USDA grant in 2007 and launched the Appalachian Staple Foods Collaborative in 2009. Community volunteers, lenders and friends helped them build the first certified organic mill in Ohio in 2010. Shagbark emphasizes that local grain systems require collaboration between farmers, millers, bakers and consumers.

Aggregate Models

These models pool resources—shared kitchens, incubator hubs and combined grocery/CSA offerings—to make local food production and distribution more efficient and accessible.

Shared-use kitchen

ACEnet & Nelsonville Food Hub

ACEnet (Appalachian Center for Economic Networks) operates the Athens Food Ventures Center—one of the first shared‑use commercial kitchens in the U.S.—and the Nelsonville Food Hub and Business Incubator. The Ventures Center houses a central kitchen, dry packaging room, thermal processing room and warehouse. Approximately 65 food entrepreneurs per year use the facility, which holds multiple licenses for food service, cold storage and frozen food operations.

As a business incubator, ACEnet provides affordable production space, cooler and freezer storage, and assistance with product development and licensing. Through the Appalachian Accessible Food Network—a partnership with Rural Action and Community Food Initiatives—ACEnet helps accelerate distribution of local foods in Appalachian Ohio, strengthening access, production and consumption while reducing barriers for startups.

Production Space

Shared kitchens and packaging rooms let entrepreneurs scale without massive capital investment.

Food Hub Access

Shared distribution infrastructure connects rural producers with regional buyers.

Community kitchen + co‑op

Local Roots Market & Café

Local Roots is a cooperatively‑owned, brick‑and‑mortar, consignment model grocery store that sells products from more than 300 local producers. Established in 2009 by volunteers, it transformed an empty building into a vibrant marketplace and features a commercial kitchen available for rent. This shared‑use kitchen supports small food businesses and supplies the in‑house café, enabling local producers to expand without significant overhead.

Consignment & Community Kitchen

Products at Local Roots are sold on consignment, so producers retain ownership and set their own prices. The model allows farmers to stay in their fields while goods are sold year‑round. The commercial kitchen provides a licensed space for entrepreneurs and is used to prepare meals served in the café. Members pay a $50 annual fee to support operations and gain a voice in the co‑op’s governance.

Aggregated CSA model

Fresh Fork Market

Fresh Fork Market is a weekly subscription service connecting more than 100 family farms within 75 miles of Cleveland to consumers. Members choose from omnivore, carnivore, vegetarian or vegan bags and pick up a weekly box of groceries at over 20 locations. Each box contains seasonal produce, pasture‑raised meats, whole grains, eggs, dairy and value‑added items.

  • Weekly subscriptions create consistent farm income.
  • Bag options serve diverse household needs.
  • Pickups across 20+ locations improve access.

Over 15 years, Fresh Fork has built relationships with farmers and refined its distribution to serve the community, effectively functioning as a modern CSA with flexible pickup points.

Butcher + grocery hybrid

Ohio City Provisions

Ohio City Provisions combines a whole‑animal butcher shop with a local grocery. The butcher features freshly cut, pasture‑raised meats and sausages from heritage breed pork and pasture‑raised chicken, while the grocery stocks all‑local goods: seasonal produce, whole grains, flours, dried beans, sourdough breads, eggs, yogurts, milk, fermented foods and canned goods.

By sourcing exclusively from nearby farmers and producers, the shop shortens the supply chain and ensures fair pay to growers. The model demonstrates how small urban retailers can sustain local agriculture through direct purchasing and transparent pricing.

Waste‑to‑Value Agriculture

Turning waste streams into valuable inputs closes loops in the food system, reduces landfill waste and builds soil fertility. These enterprises demonstrate that byproducts can become assets.

Urban compost loop

Rust Belt Riders

Rust Belt Riders is a Cleveland‑based service that collects organic waste from restaurants and redistributes it to local farms and gardens as fertilizer. The company began with co‑founders Daniel Brown and Michael Robinson riding a bicycle with a trailer to pick up food scraps from restaurants. By turning waste into compost, they close the loop between urban eateries and regional farms.

Scaling Up Through Policy & Partnership

Composting at scale required a Class II composting permit and a suitable site. Rust Belt Riders worked with the Ohio EPA to expand the exemption for community compost piles from 300 to 500 square feet, enabling more gardens to compost legally. They later partnered with Kurtz Brothers to secure the Cloverleaf Composting Facility, allowing them to produce more compost in one month than they previously could in a year. Their potting mixes have been ranked among the best in research trials and they were named Best Small‑Scale Composter in the U.S.

“Close the loop between restaurants and farms by returning food waste as soil.”

Waste-to-value agriculture

Rosby Companies

Rosby Companies operate an agricultural, horticultural and recycling business on a 70‑acre site in Brooklyn Heights near Cleveland. They produce their own soils, mulches, organic humus and compost, and run a resource recycling program that reclaims construction and demolition debris from the waste stream. The operation integrates farming with environmental stewardship by turning waste into inputs for local growers.

Compost + Soil Blends

Locally produced mulches and humus keep nutrients cycling within the region while lowering costs for growers who need soil amendments.

Recycling Hub

Construction and demolition debris is diverted from landfills to feed reclaimed material and compost streams.

Regional Grocery Chains

Regional grocers can champion local agriculture by forging direct relationships with farmers and highlighting seasonal products. These chains illustrate how scale can support, rather than undermine, local systems.

Pasture-to-plate model

The Farmer’s Rail

The Farmer’s Rail grew out of Brunty Farms, where Jeff and Melanie Brunty began raising pasture‑raised animals nearly 20 years ago to offer neighbors meat they could trust. As their farm expanded, they realized the need to control processing and opened a farmer‑owned butcher shop in 2018. Today the Farmer’s Rail includes restaurants with from‑scratch kitchens and continues to supply Brunty Farms’ meats alongside products from other local vendors.

This vertical integration—from pasture to butcher to table—ensures transparency and fair compensation to farmers while providing consumers with high‑quality, locally sourced meals. The business model also builds community around food, celebrating the connection between farmers and diners.

Integrated Supply Chain

Farmer‑owned processing and restaurants keep margins local instead of external.

Retail partnerships

Heinen’s

Heinen’s is a regional grocery chain that has long supported local farmers. Each summer, their produce departments transform into daily farmers markets, featuring the best fruits and vegetables from local fields. The company maintains relationships with farmers it has been buying from for decades, ensuring a reliable market for growers.

Heinen’s buyers frequently travel to Wayne County to purchase produce from Amish growers at auctions, and selections often arrive in stores the next morning. This direct farm‑to‑store supply chain minimizes handling and preserves freshness, demonstrating how a larger retailer can sustain local agriculture.

Auction-backed sourcing

Buehler’s Fresh Foods

Family‑owned since 1932, Buehler’s Fresh Foods is an Ohio grocery chain recognized for supporting local farmers. The company provides locally grown produce and natural meats from providers such as Gerber Poultry and the Mt. Hope Farmer’s Produce Auction. Buehler’s is the largest purchaser at the Mt. Hope auction, helping sustain growers from Holmes and Wayne counties.

Largest Mt. Hope Buyer

Anchor purchasing keeps Amish and small‑scale growers moving steady volume.

This commitment to sourcing from nearby farmers not only guarantees freshness but also keeps dollars within rural communities. Buehler’s history of community engagement illustrates how a mid‑sized retailer can anchor local food systems.
